Solar Panel Basics: How It Works

Here is the simple version. Solar panels convert sunlight into electricity. That electricity powers your home. Any extra electricity your panels produce goes back to the grid and your utility company gives you a credit on your bill. At night or on cloudy days, you pull electricity from the grid like normal but those credits offset the cost.

That is called net metering and it is how most residential solar systems work. Your electric meter literally spins backwards when your panels produce more than you use. Over the course of a year, many solar homes end up with electric bills of $0-20 per month (the $10-20 is a connection fee most utilities charge regardless of usage).

The Components

  • Solar panels: Go on your roof. Convert sunlight to DC electricity.
  • Inverter: Converts DC electricity from panels to AC electricity your home uses.
  • Racking: The mounting system that attaches panels to your roof.
  • Monitoring system: Shows you how much electricity your panels produce in real time.
  • Battery (optional): Stores excess energy for nighttime use or power outages.

Get Multiple Quotes

Solar pricing varies significantly between installers. Always get at least 3 quotes. Use sites like EnergySage to compare quotes from pre-screened installers in your area. The difference between the highest and lowest quote for the same system can be $5,000-10,000. Do not accept the first offer from a door-to-door salesperson. They are almost always overpriced.

Tax Credits and Incentives

Federal Solar Tax Credit (ITC)

The federal Investment Tax Credit gives you a 30% credit on the total cost of your solar system. This includes equipment, installation labor, permits, and even battery storage if you add it. The credit is a dollar-for-dollar reduction in your federal income tax.

Example: Your system costs $20,000. The 30% credit is $6,000. If you owe $8,000 in federal income tax that year, you now owe $2,000. If your tax liability is less than the credit amount, you can roll the remaining credit forward to future years.

The 30% rate is locked in through 2032 under the Inflation Reduction Act signed in 2022. It drops to 26% in 2033 and 22% in 2034. No income limits. No maximum credit amount. This is the most generous solar incentive in US history.

State and Local Incentives

Many states offer additional incentives on top of the federal credit. These vary widely:

  • State tax credits: Additional credits ranging from $500 to $5,000+
  • Property tax exemptions: Many states exempt solar from property tax increases
  • Sales tax exemptions: Several states exempt solar equipment from sales tax
  • Performance payments: Some states pay you per kWh your system produces (SRECs)
  • Utility rebates: Some utilities offer $500-2,000 rebates for going solar

Check the Database of State Incentives for Renewables and Efficiency (DSIRE) at dsireusa.org for incentives specific to your state and utility.

Inverters: The Brain of Your System

The inverter converts DC electricity from your panels to AC electricity your home uses. It is the second most important component after the panels themselves. There are two main types.

String Inverters

One central inverter handles all panels. Simple, reliable, and cheaper. The downside is that if one panel underperforms (shade, dirt, malfunction), it can reduce the output of the entire string. Best for roofs with no shading and panels all facing the same direction. Brands to look for: SolarEdge, Fronius, SMA.

Microinverters

A small inverter on each individual panel. Each panel operates independently so shade or issues on one panel do not affect the others. More expensive but better for roofs with partial shading, multiple orientations, or complex layouts. Also makes it easy to monitor individual panel performance and expand the system later. The leading brand is Enphase, which dominates the residential microinverter market.

Which Should You Choose?

If your roof has full sun all day and panels will all face the same direction, a string inverter with optimizers (like SolarEdge) is the best value. If your roof has any shade issues, multiple orientations, or you want the simplest monitoring, go with Enphase microinverters. Most installers will recommend the right choice for your specific roof.

Do You Need a Battery?

Battery storage is the fastest-growing part of residential solar. But does it make financial sense for you?

When a Battery Makes Sense

  • Frequent power outages: A battery keeps your lights on when the grid goes down
  • Time-of-use rates: If your utility charges more for electricity in the evening, a battery lets you use stored solar instead of expensive grid power
  • No net metering: If your utility does not offer full net metering credits, a battery lets you use more of your own solar instead of sending it to the grid for reduced credit
  • Energy independence: If reducing grid dependence is a personal priority

When a Battery Does Not Make Sense

  • Full net metering available: If your utility credits you at full retail rate for excess solar, the grid is essentially acting as a free battery
  • Rare power outages: If your grid is reliable, the backup power benefit is minimal
  • Budget-constrained: Batteries add $8,000-15,000 to your system cost. That money often generates better returns if invested in more panels instead

The most popular home batteries in 2026 are the Tesla Powerwall 3 ($8,000-10,000 installed), Enphase IQ Battery 5P ($7,000-9,000 installed), and the Franklin WholePower ($10,000-14,000 installed). All qualify for the 30% federal tax credit when installed with solar panels.

Installation: What to Expect

The installation process is simpler than most people expect. Here is the timeline from start to finish.

  1. Get quotes (Week 1-2): Contact 3+ installers or use EnergySage. Compare pricing, equipment, and warranties.
  2. Sign contract (Week 2-3): Choose your installer. Sign the agreement. Put down the deposit (typically 10-20%).
  3. Site survey (Week 3-4): The installer visits your home, inspects the roof, checks the electrical panel, and finalizes the system design.
  4. Permitting (Week 4-8): Your installer handles all permits with your city or county. This is often the longest wait. Some jurisdictions take 2-4 weeks.
  5. Installation day (Week 8-10): A crew of 2-4 workers installs the panels, racking, inverter, and wiring in 1-2 days for most residential systems.
  6. Inspection (Week 10-11): The city inspector verifies the installation meets code.
  7. Utility approval (Week 11-14): Your utility installs a new meter (if needed) and gives permission to operate. This can take 1-4 weeks depending on the utility.
  8. Turn on (Week 12-14): Flip the switch. Start generating free electricity.

Total time from signing to generating: typically 8-14 weeks. The actual installation day is just 1-2 days. Most of the timeline is permitting and utility approval.

Cash purchase gives you the highest total savings. You own the system, get the tax credit, and have no monthly payments. After payback (6-9 years), everything is pure savings.

Solar loan lets you go solar with $0 down while still owning the system and getting the tax credit. Monthly loan payments are often less than your old electricity bill, so you save from day one. After the loan is paid off, you get the full benefit of free electricity.

Lease or PPA means a company puts panels on your roof and you pay them a monthly fee (lease) or a per-kWh rate (PPA) that is lower than your utility rate. You save some money but the company keeps most of the financial benefit. Leases can complicate home sales and usually include annual escalation clauses that increase your rate 1-3% per year.

Is Solar Right for Your Home?

Solar makes great financial sense for most homeowners, but not all. Here is how to figure out if you are a good candidate.

Solar Is Probably Right for You If:

  • You own your home
  • Your electricity bill is $100+/month
  • Your roof gets good sun (south, east, or west facing)
  • Your roof is in good condition and will not need replacement in the next 10 years
  • You plan to stay in the home for 5+ years
  • Your state has net metering

Solar Might Not Be Right If:

  • You rent your home
  • Your electricity bill is under $50/month
  • Your roof is heavily shaded by trees or buildings
  • Your roof needs replacement soon (do the roof first, then solar)
  • You plan to move within 2-3 years
  • You live in an area with extremely cheap electricity (under $0.08/kWh)

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do solar panels cost for a home in 2026?

$15,000-25,000 before the 30% federal tax credit for a typical 6-10 kW system. After the credit, $10,500-17,500. Per-watt costs range from $2.50-3.50 installed. Always get 3+ quotes to compare pricing.

How long do solar panels take to pay for themselves?

6-9 years on average. Faster in high-electricity-cost states (4-6 years). Slower in low-cost areas (10-12 years). After payback, you get 15-20 years of essentially free electricity.

What is the federal solar tax credit?

30% of the total system cost as a dollar-for-dollar federal tax credit. Locked at 30% through 2032. No income limit and no maximum credit amount. Covers panels, inverter, battery, installation, and permits.

Do solar panels work on cloudy days?

Yes. Panels produce 10-25% output on overcast days and 50-80% on partly cloudy days. Systems are designed based on annual averages. Net metering credits from sunny days offset cloudy days.

Should you buy or lease solar panels?

Buying is almost always better financially. You get the tax credit, own the equipment, and keep all the savings. If you cannot pay cash, a solar loan is usually better than a lease. Leases can complicate home sales.

How long do solar panels last?

25-30+ years. Most manufacturers warranty 25 years at 80-85% output. Degradation is about 0.5% per year. Inverters last 10-15 years and may need one replacement. Solar panels are one of the most durable home investments available.
