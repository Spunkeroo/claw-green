How to Start Composting at Home: Beginner's Guide

Every year, the average American household throws away about 400 pounds of food scraps. Those banana peels, coffee grounds, and vegetable trimmings go straight into a plastic bag, into a garbage truck, and into a landfill where they rot and produce methane, a greenhouse gas that is about 80 times more potent than carbon dioxide in the short term.

Composting changes all of that. Instead of sending your food scraps to a landfill, you turn them into rich, dark, nutrient-packed soil that your garden absolutely loves. It is free fertilizer that you make from stuff you were going to throw away anyway.

The best part? Composting at home is way easier than most people think. You do not need a huge yard. You do not need expensive equipment. You do not even need to go outside if you do not want to. This guide covers everything from the simplest method for total beginners to more advanced techniques for people who want compost faster.

What Composting Actually Is (The Simple Version)

Composting is just controlled decomposition. You take organic stuff (food scraps, leaves, cardboard) and let microorganisms break it down into soil. That is literally it. Nature does this on its own in every forest on earth. Leaves fall, they decompose, they become soil. Composting is just doing the same thing in a more organized way.

The end product is called compost, or sometimes "black gold" by gardeners. It looks like dark, crumbly soil and smells like a forest after rain. It is packed with nutrients that plants love and it improves soil structure, water retention, and drainage. It is genuinely one of the best things you can add to any garden.

For composting to work, you need four things:

  • Greens: Nitrogen-rich materials like food scraps, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings, and fresh plant trimmings.
  • Browns: Carbon-rich materials like dry leaves, cardboard, newspaper, straw, wood chips, and dryer lint.
  • Water: The pile needs to be damp like a wrung-out sponge. Not soaking wet, not dry.
  • Air: The microorganisms that do the work need oxygen. That is why you turn the pile.

The ideal ratio is roughly 3 parts brown to 1 part green by volume. You do not need to be exact. Think of it like cooking. A little more or less of something is fine. If the pile smells bad, add more browns. If nothing is happening, add more greens. Easy.

What You Can and Cannot Compost

Greens (Nitrogen-Rich) - Yes, Compost These

  • Fruit and vegetable scraps (peels, cores, tops, rinds)
  • Coffee grounds and paper coffee filters
  • Tea bags (remove staples, skip synthetic bags)
  • Fresh grass clippings
  • Plant trimmings and weeds (before they seed)
  • Eggshells (crush them for faster breakdown)
  • Old bread and grains

Browns (Carbon-Rich) - Yes, Compost These

  • Dry leaves
  • Cardboard (torn into small pieces, remove tape)
  • Newspaper (shredded)
  • Paper towels and napkins (unbleached preferred)
  • Straw and hay
  • Wood chips and sawdust (untreated wood only)
  • Dryer lint (from natural fiber clothes only)
  • Dead houseplants and dried flowers

Do NOT Compost These

  • Meat, fish, and bones (attracts pests and rodents)
  • Dairy products (attracts pests, creates odor)
  • Cooking oils and grease
  • Pet waste from dogs and cats (contains harmful pathogens)
  • Diseased plants (can spread disease to your garden)
  • Treated or painted wood
  • Coal or charcoal ash
  • Glossy or coated paper
  • Weeds that have gone to seed
  • Plants treated with pesticides or herbicides

Setting Up an Outdoor Compost Bin

If you have a yard, this is the simplest way to start. You can go as basic as a pile in the corner of your yard, but a bin keeps things neater and faster.

Option 1: Buy a Compost Bin

A basic outdoor compost bin costs $30-80 and is the fastest way to get started. Look for one that is at least 3 feet by 3 feet by 3 feet. This size retains enough heat for active decomposition. Smaller bins work but take longer.

For faster composting, a compost tumbler is a great upgrade. It is a sealed drum on a frame that you spin to turn the compost. No pitchfork needed. Tumblers cost $80-150 but they produce compost in 4-8 weeks instead of months.

Option 2: DIY Bin

You can make a compost bin from almost anything. The most common DIY options:

  • Wire mesh bin: Bend hardware cloth or chicken wire into a circle, secure with zip ties. Takes 5 minutes and costs under $20.
  • Pallet bin: Stand four wooden pallets upright and wire them together to form a box. Free if you can find pallets.
  • Plastic storage bin: Drill holes in a large plastic storage tote (lid and sides) for airflow. Works for small batches.

Where to Place Your Bin

Pick a spot that has:

  • Partial shade (full sun dries it out, full shade slows it down)
  • Good drainage (not a spot where water pools)
  • Easy access from your kitchen (you are more likely to compost if it is convenient)
  • Direct contact with bare soil (so worms and microorganisms can get in)

Getting Started

  1. Put a 4-6 inch layer of browns on the bottom (dry leaves, straw, or shredded cardboard). This creates airflow at the base.
  2. Add a layer of greens (kitchen scraps, coffee grounds).
  3. Add another layer of browns on top. Always cover food scraps with browns to prevent odors and flies.
  4. Sprinkle with water until damp but not soggy.
  5. Keep adding layers as you generate scraps. Always top with browns.

Kitchen Collection Tip

Keep a small countertop compost bin in your kitchen for collecting scraps throughout the day. The ones with charcoal filters prevent any smell. Empty it into your outdoor bin every few days. This single habit makes composting practically automatic.

Indoor Composting for Apartments

No yard? No problem. Here are three methods that work perfectly in apartments.

Vermicomposting (Worm Bin)

This is the most popular indoor composting method. Red wiggler worms eat your food scraps and produce worm castings, which is basically supercharged compost. A worm bin fits under your kitchen sink, in a closet, or on a balcony.

You need a worm composting bin ($30-60) and about 1 pound of red wiggler worms ($25-35). The worms eat roughly half their body weight in food scraps per day. A pound of worms handles about half a pound of scraps daily, which is plenty for most households.

Setup is simple: shredded newspaper on the bottom for bedding, add the worms, and start feeding them small amounts of kitchen scraps. Avoid citrus, onions, and spicy food (worms do not like them). The bin should not smell at all when maintained properly.

Bokashi Composting

Bokashi is a Japanese fermentation method. You layer food scraps with Bokashi bran (inoculated with beneficial microorganisms) in a sealed bucket. It ferments for 2 weeks, then you bury the pre-compost in soil where it finishes decomposing in another 2-4 weeks.

The big advantage of Bokashi is that you can compost everything including meat, dairy, and cooked food. A Bokashi starter kit costs about $40-50.

Electric Composters

If you want zero effort, electric composters like the Lomi or FoodCycler grind, heat, and dehydrate food scraps into a soil amendment in 4-24 hours. They are the most expensive option ($300-500) but they are completely hands-off. Add scraps, press a button, get compost. They are quiet and odorless.

Maintaining Your Compost

Once your compost bin is set up, maintenance is minimal. Here is what you need to do:

Turn It Regularly

Turning introduces oxygen, which speeds up decomposition and prevents the pile from going anaerobic (which causes smell). Turn your pile every 1-2 weeks with a pitchfork or compost aerator tool. If using a tumbler, just give it a few spins every few days.

Keep It Moist

The pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge. If it is too dry, decomposition slows to a crawl. If it is too wet, it gets slimy and smelly. In dry weather, add water when you turn it. In rainy weather, cover it with a tarp or lid.

Balance Greens and Browns

If the pile smells like ammonia or rotten food, there is too much green. Add browns (shredded cardboard, dry leaves). If nothing seems to be happening and the pile is dry and unchanged, there is too much brown. Add greens (food scraps, fresh grass) and water.

How to Know When Compost Is Ready

Finished compost is dark brown, crumbly, and smells like earth. You should not be able to recognize any of the original materials. It usually takes 2-6 months depending on your method and how actively you manage it. If there are still recognizable chunks, just screen them out and toss them back in for another round.

Troubleshooting Common Problems

The compost smells bad

This is the number one concern people have, and it is almost always caused by too much green material (food scraps) and not enough brown material. Add a thick layer of shredded cardboard or dry leaves on top. Turn the pile to introduce air. Within a day or two the smell should be gone. Also make sure you are not adding meat, dairy, or oil.

The compost is attracting flies

Fruit flies love exposed food scraps. Always bury new food scraps under a layer of browns. Keep a bag of shredded newspaper or dry leaves near your bin and add a handful on top every time you add food. If flies are already a problem, add a thick 3-4 inch layer of browns on top.

The compost is not breaking down

The pile might be too dry, too small, or missing greens. Add water and fresh nitrogen-rich materials. Chop or shred large pieces before adding them (a whole apple takes months to decompose, a chopped apple takes weeks). Make sure your bin is at least 3 feet in each dimension. Smaller piles do not retain enough heat.

Animals are getting into it

If raccoons, rats, or other animals are raiding your bin, switch to a fully enclosed bin or tumbler. Never add meat, dairy, or cooked food to an open pile. A layer of hardware cloth on the bottom of your bin prevents burrowing animals from getting in from below.

The compost is slimy and wet

Too much moisture and not enough air. Turn the pile vigorously to break up any clumps. Add dry browns like shredded cardboard or sawdust. If your bin does not have drainage, drill holes in the bottom. In rainy areas, keep a lid or tarp over the pile.

Using Your Finished Compost

You have waited patiently and now you have beautiful, dark, crumbly compost. Here is how to use it:

  • Garden beds: Mix 2-3 inches of compost into the top 6 inches of soil before planting. This is the best thing you can do for any garden.
  • Top dressing: Spread a 1-inch layer of compost around existing plants and on your lawn. Water it in.
  • Potting mix: Mix one part compost with one part garden soil and one part perlite or vermiculite for a great homemade potting mix.
  • Compost tea: Steep finished compost in water for 24-48 hours. Strain and use the liquid to water plants. It is like a vitamin shot for your garden.
  • Seed starting: Sifted compost mixed with vermiculite makes an excellent seed starting medium.

You literally cannot add too much compost to a garden. Unlike synthetic fertilizers, compost will not burn plants. Use as much as you can make.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to make compost at home?

Basic composting takes 2-6 months. Hot composting (actively managed) can produce finished compost in 4-8 weeks. Cold composting (pile it up and leave it) takes 6-12 months. Vermicomposting takes about 3-6 months.

Does composting at home smell bad?

A properly maintained compost pile should not smell bad. If it smells, add more browns (dry leaves, cardboard) and turn the pile. A healthy compost pile smells like earthy forest floor.

Can you compost in an apartment without a yard?

Yes. Worm bins fit under your kitchen sink. Bokashi composting uses sealed buckets. Electric composters like Lomi process scraps in hours. All work in apartments with minimal smell.

What should you not put in a compost bin?

Do not compost meat, fish, dairy, oils, pet waste, diseased plants, treated wood, or coal ash. These attract pests, create odors, or introduce harmful chemicals.

Do you need worms to compost?

No. Traditional composting uses bacteria and fungi. Vermicomposting with worms is a separate method that works great indoors but is optional for outdoor composting.

Is composting actually worth it?

Yes. The average household throws away 30% of its waste as food scraps and yard waste. Composting diverts this from landfills, produces free fertilizer, and improves your garden. A basic bin costs $30-50 and pays for itself within a season.
