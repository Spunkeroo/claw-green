Best Reusable Products to Replace Single-Use Plastic (2026)

Here is a number that is hard to wrap your head around: humans produce about 400 million tons of plastic waste every single year. Most of it gets used once and thrown away. That water bottle you drank from for 20 minutes? It will exist on this planet for 450 years.

The good news is that swapping out single-use plastic for reusable products is one of the easiest, cheapest, and most impactful things you can do. Most swaps cost under $20, last for years, and save you money in the long run. You do not need to overhaul your entire life overnight. Just start with a few key swaps and build from there.

This guide covers the best reusable alternatives for every single-use plastic product in your daily life, from water bottles to food storage to cleaning supplies. Every product recommended here has been tested, reviewed by thousands of users, and actually works in real life, not just in theory.

The Real Impact of Single-Use Plastic

Before we get into products, let us look at the actual numbers. This is not about guilt. It is about understanding where the biggest opportunities are so you can focus your swaps where they matter most.

  • Plastic bottles: Americans buy 50 billion water bottles per year. Only 30% get recycled. The rest end up in landfills or the ocean.
  • Plastic bags: The average American family takes home almost 1,500 plastic bags per year. They are used for an average of 12 minutes.
  • Coffee cups: Over 16 billion disposable coffee cups are used globally every year. Most are lined with plastic and cannot be recycled.
  • Food packaging: About 40% of all plastic produced is for packaging that is used once and discarded.

The average person generates about 100 pounds of plastic waste per year. The good news? You can eliminate most of that with about 10-15 reusable products that cost a total of $100-200 and last for years.

Reusable Water Bottles

This is the single biggest swap you can make. If you buy bottled water, switching to a reusable bottle saves you $200-300 per year and eliminates hundreds of plastic bottles from the waste stream.

Best Overall: Insulated Stainless Steel

Insulated stainless steel bottles keep water cold for 24 hours and hot drinks warm for 12 hours. They do not retain flavors, are virtually indestructible, and last a lifetime. Brands like Hydro Flask, Iron Flask, and Simple Modern all make excellent options. Expect to pay $15-35 depending on size.

Best Budget: Iron Flask or Simple Modern

You do not need to spend $40+ on a Hydro Flask. Iron Flask and Simple Modern both make insulated bottles that perform identically for $15-20. Over 100,000 five-star reviews on Amazon. They come in every size and color imaginable.

Best for the Gym: Wide Mouth with Straw Lid

Get a wide-mouth bottle with a straw lid for easy one-handed drinking. Wide mouth also makes it easy to add ice cubes. 32oz is the sweet spot for most people. Wide mouth bottles with straw lids start at about $15.

Pro Tip

Get a bottle brush for weekly deep cleaning. A quick rinse with hot soapy water daily keeps it fresh, but a bottle brush set ($6-8) gets into the bottom corners where bacteria can build up.

Reusable Shopping Bags

Most states now charge for plastic bags, so you are probably already partway there. But the trick is having reusable bags that are convenient enough that you actually remember to bring them.

Best for Groceries: Foldable Reusable Bags

Foldable reusable grocery bags that compress into a tiny pouch you can clip to your keys or toss in the glove compartment. When you need them, they unfold to full-size bags that hold way more than a plastic bag. About $10-15 for a set of 5-6. Machine washable.

Best for Produce: Mesh Produce Bags

Those little plastic bags in the produce section? Replace them with reusable mesh produce bags. They are lightweight (so they do not throw off the scale), see-through (so the cashier can ID your produce), and machine washable. A set of 9-12 costs about $10 and lasts for years.

The biggest challenge with reusable bags is remembering to bring them. Keep them in your car, clip one to your everyday bag, and keep a couple by the front door. After a few weeks it becomes automatic.

Reusable Food Storage

Plastic wrap, sandwich bags, and disposable containers are some of the most common single-use plastics in the kitchen. Fortunately, there are great alternatives for all of them.

Replace Zip-Lock Bags: Silicone Food Storage Bags

Silicone food storage bags work exactly like zip-lock bags but they are reusable, dishwasher safe, freezer safe, and microwave safe. Brands like Stasher are popular but there are plenty of affordable options for $10-15 per set. They seal airtight and work for everything from snacks to marinating meat.

Replace Plastic Wrap: Beeswax Wraps

Beeswax wraps replace plastic wrap for covering bowls, wrapping cheese, and storing half-used fruits and vegetables. They stick to themselves and to bowls using the warmth of your hands. A set of 3-4 wraps costs $12-18 and lasts about a year before needing replacement. Wash with cool water (heat melts the wax).

Replace Disposable Containers: Glass Food Storage

Glass food storage containers are better than plastic in every way. They do not stain, do not absorb odors, are microwave and oven safe, and last basically forever. A set of 10-12 containers with locking lids costs $25-40. Pyrex and Glasslock are the most popular brands.

Reusable Coffee Cups and Mugs

Disposable coffee cups seem like they should be recyclable because they are made of paper, but most of them have a thin plastic lining that makes them non-recyclable. If you buy coffee 3-5 times a week, that is 150-250 disposable cups per year from you alone.

Best Travel Mug: Insulated Stainless Steel

An insulated travel mug keeps your coffee hot for hours and fits in most car cup holders. Look for one with a leak-proof lid. Contigo, Zojirushi, and Simple Modern all make excellent options for $15-30. Many coffee shops give you a $0.25-0.50 discount when you bring your own cup, so it pays for itself quickly.

Best for Iced Coffee: Reusable Iced Coffee Cup

If iced coffee is your thing, get a reusable iced coffee cup with a straw. They look just like the disposable ones but are made from durable BPA-free plastic or glass. About $10-15 each.

Reusable Straws and Utensils

Reusable Straws

Stainless steel straws are the most popular and durable option. They come with cleaning brushes and last forever. If you prefer a softer feel, silicone straws are bendable and gentle on teeth. A set of 8 metal straws with brushes costs about $6-8.

Reusable Utensil Set

Keep a reusable utensil travel set in your bag for eating on the go. Most sets include a fork, knife, spoon, chopsticks, and straw in a carrying case. Under $10 for a set. Skip the plastic cutlery at takeout places.

Kitchen Swaps (Paper Towels, Wrap, Sponges)

Replace Paper Towels: Reusable Cloths

The average American household uses 80+ rolls of paper towels per year. That is over $100 in paper towels and a lot of waste. Swedish dishcloths are the best alternative. Each one replaces 15-17 rolls of paper towels, is machine washable, and composts at end of life. A 10-pack costs about $15 and lasts a year.

For bigger messes, keep a stack of microfiber cleaning cloths under the sink. Toss them in the laundry when dirty. A 24-pack costs about $12 and lasts for years.

Replace Plastic Sponges: Natural Alternatives

Traditional sponges are made from plastic foam that sheds microplastics. Switch to natural cellulose sponges or coconut coir scrubbers. They work just as well, are biodegradable, and cost about the same. Walnut shell scrub pads are great for tough stuck-on food.

Replace Aluminum Foil: Silicone Baking Mats

Silicone baking mats replace aluminum foil and parchment paper for baking. They are non-stick, oven-safe up to 480F, and last for thousands of uses. A set of 3 costs about $12-15.

Bathroom and Personal Care Swaps

The bathroom is a huge source of single-use plastic that most people overlook.

Replace Plastic Toothbrushes: Bamboo Toothbrushes

You replace your toothbrush every 3 months, which means 4 plastic toothbrushes per year per person going to landfill. Bamboo toothbrushes work identically but the handle composts. A 10-pack costs about $8-10, enough for over 2 years.

Replace Liquid Soap Bottles: Bar Soap and Shampoo Bars

Every liquid soap, shampoo, and conditioner comes in a plastic bottle. Switch to bar versions. Shampoo bars last as long as 2-3 bottles of liquid shampoo and come in paper or cardboard packaging. Brands like Ethique and HiBAR make bars for every hair type.

Replace Disposable Razors: Safety Razor

A stainless steel safety razor costs $20-30 and replacement blades cost about $0.10 each (compared to $3-5 per cartridge for disposable razors). The shave is better, it costs less over time, and you eliminate all that plastic packaging. The upfront cost pays for itself within 3-4 months.

Replace Cotton Rounds: Reusable Makeup Pads

Reusable makeup remover pads are soft, machine washable, and work just like disposable cotton rounds. A set of 16-20 pads with a laundry bag costs about $10-12 and lasts for years.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are reusable products actually better for the environment?

Yes. A reusable water bottle needs about 10-20 uses to offset its manufacturing impact. Since most last for years and get used hundreds of times, the environmental savings are significant. The key is actually using them consistently.

What are the easiest single-use plastic swaps to start with?

Start with four high-impact swaps: a reusable water bottle, reusable shopping bags, a reusable coffee cup, and reusable food storage containers. These alone can eliminate hundreds of single-use items per year.

How much money can you save by switching to reusable products?

The average household spends $200-400 per year on single-use products. Reusable alternatives cost $100-200 upfront but last 2-5 years. That is over $3,000 saved in 5 years.

What is the best reusable water bottle material?

Stainless steel is the best all-around choice. It does not retain flavors, does not leach chemicals, is nearly indestructible, and keeps drinks cold for 24+ hours when insulated.

How do you keep reusable products clean?

Most are dishwasher safe. Wash water bottles daily with hot soapy water and deep clean weekly with a bottle brush. Silicone bags hand wash with cool water. Shopping bags go in the washing machine monthly.

Are reusable straws worth it?

If you use straws regularly, yes. The average American uses 580 disposable straws per year. A set of reusable straws costs $6-8 and eliminates all of that waste forever.
