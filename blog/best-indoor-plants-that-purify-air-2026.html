Best Indoor Plants That Purify Air in 2026: 20 NASA-Backed Picks

Indoor air is typically 2-5 times more polluted than outdoor air according to the EPA. Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from furniture, paint, cleaning products, and building materials accumulate in enclosed spaces. Air-purifying plants offer a natural, low-cost way to reduce these toxins while adding humidity, reducing stress, and improving the look of your home.

NASA's Clean Air Study, conducted in 1989, tested 50 common houseplants for their ability to remove formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene, and ammonia from sealed chambers. The results identified specific plants that are exceptionally effective at absorbing these common indoor pollutants. This guide presents the 20 best air-purifying plants based on that research and subsequent studies, with practical care information for each one.

Every plant on this list includes the specific toxins it removes, its light and water requirements, care difficulty rating, whether it is safe for pets, and the best room placement. We organized them from easiest to care for to most demanding, so beginners can start at the top and experienced plant owners can explore the full list.

How Plants Purify Indoor Air

Plants clean air through three mechanisms. First, the leaves absorb gaseous pollutants through their stomata (tiny pores on the leaf surface) during normal gas exchange. The plant metabolizes these chemicals, breaking them down into harmless compounds used for growth. Second, microorganisms in the soil around the plant's roots break down organic chemicals that settle into the soil. Third, plants release water vapor through transpiration, which increases humidity and helps airborne particles settle out of the breathing zone.

The five most common indoor air pollutants that plants can remove are:

  • Formaldehyde: Released by furniture, flooring, insulation, and cleaning products. The most common indoor VOC.
  • Benzene: Found in paints, glues, detergents, and tobacco smoke. A known carcinogen.
  • Trichloroethylene: Present in dry cleaning solvents, adhesives, and paint removers.
  • Xylene: Emitted by paints, varnishes, adhesives, and printing materials.
  • Ammonia: Found in cleaning products, fertilizers, and some building materials.

Easy Care Air-Purifying Plants (Beginner Friendly)

These plants thrive with minimal attention. They tolerate irregular watering, low light, and general neglect. If you have never kept a houseplant alive, start here.

1. Snake Plant (Sansevieria trifasciata)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene | Light: Low to bright indirect | Water: Every 2-6 weeks | Pet safe: No (mildly toxic)

The Snake Plant is the most forgiving houseplant in existence. It thrives in every light condition from near darkness to bright sun, needs water only when the soil is completely dry (every 2-6 weeks depending on season), and is practically immune to pests. It is one of the few plants that converts CO2 to oxygen at night, making it ideal for bedrooms. The NASA study rated it among the top performers for formaldehyde removal. A single Snake Plant in a bedroom measurably reduces nighttime CO2 levels. Available at every garden center and most grocery stores for $5-15. Browse Snake Plants on Amazon

2. Pothos (Epipremnum aureum)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene, xylene | Light: Low to bright indirect | Water: When top inch of soil is dry | Pet safe: No (toxic if ingested)

Pothos is the quintessential beginner plant. Its trailing vines grow rapidly in almost any condition, from dim offices to bright kitchens. It communicates clearly when it needs water by drooping its leaves, then perks back up within hours of watering. It grows in water alone (no soil needed), making it versatile for different placements. NASA found Pothos particularly effective at removing formaldehyde, which is the most prevalent indoor VOC. Hang it in a basket, trail it along a shelf, or grow it in a glass jar of water on a windowsill. It costs $5-10 and propagates effortlessly from cuttings.

3. ZZ Plant (Zamioculcas zamiifolia)

Toxins removed: Xylene, toluene, benzene | Light: Low to bright indirect | Water: Every 2-4 weeks | Pet safe: No (toxic if ingested)

The ZZ Plant has thick, waxy leaves that look polished without any effort. Its underground rhizomes store water like a camel, allowing it to survive months of drought. It grows in extremely low light, making it perfect for windowless bathrooms, dark corners, and interior offices. New growth emerges as bright green shoots that gradually darken to deep forest green. It is slow-growing, which means it rarely needs repotting. The glossy leaves also resist dust buildup better than most houseplants. ZZ Plants are virtually indestructible and cost $10-25 depending on size.

4. Spider Plant (Chlorophytum comosum)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, xylene, carbon monoxide | Light: Bright indirect to low | Water: When top inch of soil is dry | Pet safe: Yes (non-toxic to cats and dogs)

The Spider Plant is one of the most effective air purifiers in the NASA study and one of the safest for pet households. It removes formaldehyde and xylene at high rates and is completely non-toxic to cats and dogs. It produces cascading "babies" (spiderettes) on long stems that can be snipped and potted to create new plants. A single Spider Plant can produce dozens of babies per year, making it essentially free to propagate throughout your home. It adapts to almost any light level, though bright indirect light produces the most growth and babies. Hang it in a macrame planter for the classic look.

5. Rubber Plant (Ficus elastica)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, carbon dioxide | Light: Bright indirect | Water: When top 2 inches of soil are dry | Pet safe: No (toxic if ingested)

The Rubber Plant has large, thick, glossy leaves that are particularly effective at absorbing formaldehyde. The large leaf surface area provides more area for gas exchange than small-leaved plants. It grows into an impressive indoor tree over time, reaching 6-10 feet indoors with proper care. Available in classic dark green, burgundy, and variegated varieties. It prefers bright indirect light but tolerates medium light. Water when the top 2 inches of soil are dry. Wipe the leaves with a damp cloth monthly to remove dust and maximize their air-purifying efficiency. Costs $15-40 depending on size.

6. Dracaena (Dracaena marginata)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene | Light: Low to bright indirect | Water: When top half of soil is dry | Pet safe: No (toxic to cats and dogs)

Dracaena marginata, also called the Dragon Tree, was one of the top performers in the NASA study, removing four of the five tested pollutants. Its thin, arching leaves on tall, slender trunks give it an elegant, architectural appearance. It tolerates low light and infrequent watering, making it easy to care for. The plant is sensitive to fluoride in tap water, which can cause brown leaf tips. If your tap water is fluoridated, use filtered water. It grows slowly to 6-8 feet indoors and rarely needs attention beyond occasional watering. Available for $15-30.

7. Peace Lily (Spathiphyllum)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene, ammonia | Light: Low to medium indirect | Water: When top inch of soil is dry (droops when thirsty) | Pet safe: No (toxic if ingested)

The Peace Lily is the only plant in the NASA study that removes all five tested pollutants, including ammonia. It is also one of the few air-purifying plants that flowers indoors in low light, producing elegant white spathes. Like Pothos, it droops dramatically when it needs water and recovers quickly after watering, making it easy to know when to water. It thrives in low light conditions where most other plants would struggle. Its broad leaves and flowering habit make it one of the most attractive air purifiers. NASA ranked it as the overall best plant for removing indoor air pollutants. Costs $10-25.

The Top 3 for Maximum Air Purification

If you want to maximize air purification with minimum effort, get these three plants: Snake Plant for the bedroom (produces oxygen at night), Peace Lily for the living room (removes all 5 major toxins), and Pothos for the kitchen or bathroom (handles humidity and low light). Together, these three cost under $30 and cover the broadest range of pollutant removal with the easiest care requirements.

Moderate Care Air-Purifying Plants

These plants need more consistent watering, specific light conditions, or occasional maintenance. They reward attentive care with vigorous growth and superior air purification.

8. Boston Fern (Nephrolepis exaltata)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, xylene | Light: Bright indirect | Water: Keep soil consistently moist | Pet safe: Yes (non-toxic)

The Boston Fern is the most effective plant for removing formaldehyde from indoor air. NASA rated it number one for this specific pollutant. It also increases humidity significantly through transpiration, which benefits respiratory health in dry climates. The challenge is that it needs consistently moist soil and high humidity. It drops leaves in dry environments, which can be messy. The ideal location is a bright bathroom or kitchen where humidity stays above 50%. Mist the fronds daily or place the pot on a pebble tray with water. The extra effort is worth it if formaldehyde is your primary concern. Costs $10-20.

9. Areca Palm (Dypsis lutescens)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, xylene, toluene | Light: Bright indirect | Water: When top inch of soil is dry | Pet safe: Yes (non-toxic)

The Areca Palm is the best air-purifying plant for large rooms. A 6-foot Areca Palm can transpire up to a liter of water per day, making it a natural humidifier. It is particularly effective at removing formaldehyde from carpet, furniture, and building materials. It is also completely non-toxic to pets, making it ideal for households with cats and dogs. It needs bright indirect light and consistent watering (let the top inch dry between waterings). It grows to 6-8 feet indoors and makes a dramatic statement in living rooms and entryways. Costs $20-60 depending on size. Browse Areca Palms on Amazon

10. Bamboo Palm (Chamaedorea seifrizii)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene | Light: Low to bright indirect | Water: Keep soil evenly moist | Pet safe: Yes (non-toxic)

The Bamboo Palm scored high in the NASA study for benzene and trichloroethylene removal. It tolerates lower light than most palms, making it suitable for rooms with north-facing windows or limited natural light. It is pet-safe, grows to 4-6 feet, and has a bushy, tropical appearance that softens room aesthetics. Water regularly to keep soil evenly moist but not waterlogged. It prefers some humidity but tolerates normal household levels better than the Boston Fern. A good choice for offices, living rooms, and bedrooms.

11. Philodendron (Philodendron hederaceum)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde | Light: Low to bright indirect | Water: When top inch of soil is dry | Pet safe: No (toxic if ingested)

The Heartleaf Philodendron is a trailing plant similar to Pothos but with heart-shaped, velvety leaves. It excels at formaldehyde removal and adapts to a wide range of light conditions. It grows rapidly, with vines reaching 10+ feet, making it ideal for shelves, hanging baskets, and trellises. It is slightly more finicky than Pothos about overwatering (it prefers the soil to dry slightly between waterings) but is still very easy to grow. The deep green, heart-shaped leaves are aesthetically pleasing and complement most interior styles.

12. English Ivy (Hedera helix)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene | Light: Bright indirect to partial shade | Water: Keep soil evenly moist | Pet safe: No (toxic if ingested)

English Ivy was rated the number one plant for removing benzene in the NASA study. It also removes formaldehyde, trichloroethylene, and xylene. Research from the University of Georgia found that English Ivy was the best overall plant for air purification when considering all pollutants combined. It grows aggressively and needs regular pruning to stay manageable indoors. It prefers cooler temperatures (55-70 degrees F) and bright indirect light. The trailing habit makes it beautiful in hanging baskets. It can also be trained on a trellis or moss pole. Costs $5-15 for a starter plant.

13. Aloe Vera (Aloe barbadensis)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene | Light: Bright indirect to direct | Water: Every 2-3 weeks | Pet safe: No (mildly toxic to cats and dogs)

Aloe Vera removes formaldehyde and benzene while providing a practical benefit: the gel inside its leaves soothes burns, cuts, and skin irritations. It prefers bright light and infrequent watering, making it perfect for sunny windowsills and kitchen countertops. It produces offsets (pups) that can be separated and potted to create new plants. Overwatering is the most common cause of death; let the soil dry completely between waterings. It is drought-tolerant and stores water in its thick leaves. A useful and attractive plant that costs $5-15.

Advanced Care Air-Purifying Plants

These plants require specific conditions or more attention but provide exceptional air purification and visual impact.

14. Weeping Fig (Ficus benjamina)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, xylene, toluene | Light: Bright indirect (consistent) | Water: When top inch of soil is dry | Pet safe: No (toxic to cats and dogs)

The Weeping Fig is an effective air purifier that grows into an impressive indoor tree, reaching 6-10 feet. However, it is notoriously sensitive to environmental changes. Moving it, changing its light, or altering its watering schedule causes dramatic leaf drop. Once established in a spot with consistent bright indirect light, it thrives with minimal attention. The key is finding the right location and leaving it there. The dense foliage provides substantial air-purifying capacity. It is one of the most attractive indoor trees when healthy and well-established.

15. Chrysanthemum (Chrysanthemum morifolium)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, benzene, trichloroethylene, xylene, ammonia | Light: Bright direct | Water: Keep soil moist | Pet safe: No (toxic to cats and dogs)

The Chrysanthemum is one of only two plants in the NASA study that removes all five tested pollutants (along with the Peace Lily). It is also the most effective overall at removing these chemicals. The challenge is that chrysanthemums need bright direct sunlight to bloom, and the air-purifying benefits are greatest when the plant is flowering. They are typically treated as temporary indoor plants (4-6 weeks of bloom) and then composted or planted outdoors. Buy a new potted chrysanthemum every month or two for continuous air purification. They cost $5-10 per pot at grocery stores and garden centers.

16. Flamingo Lily (Anthurium andraeanum)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, xylene, ammonia | Light: Bright indirect | Water: When top inch of soil is dry | Pet safe: No (toxic if ingested)

The Flamingo Lily produces striking red, pink, or white waxy flowers that last for weeks. It is one of the few air-purifying plants that reliably flowers indoors. The NASA study found it effective at removing formaldehyde, xylene, and ammonia. It prefers bright indirect light, warm temperatures (65-80 degrees F), and high humidity. Weekly watering and occasional misting keep it happy. The combination of air purification and dramatic flowering makes it a popular choice for living rooms and offices.

17. Broadleaf Lady Palm (Rhapis excelsa)

Toxins removed: Formaldehyde, ammonia, xylene | Light: Low to bright indirect | Water: Keep soil evenly moist | Pet safe: Yes (non-toxic)

The Lady Palm is a slow-growing, elegant palm that excels at removing ammonia, making it ideal for kitchens and areas where cleaning products are used frequently. It tolerates lower light than most palms, adapts to a range of humidity levels, and is completely safe for pets. The fan-shaped leaves have a refined, tropical appearance. It is one of the most expensive plants on this list ($40-100) because it grows slowly and is harder to propagate commercially, but it requires minimal care once established and can live for decades indoors.

Pet-Safe Air-Purifying Plants

If you have cats or dogs, pet safety is non-negotiable. The following plants are confirmed non-toxic to cats and dogs by the ASPCA:

  • Spider Plant: Removes formaldehyde, xylene. Easy care. Produces babies for propagation.
  • Boston Fern: Top formaldehyde remover. Needs humidity. Great for bathrooms.
  • Areca Palm: Excellent humidifier. Removes formaldehyde. Bright light needed.
  • Bamboo Palm: Removes benzene and formaldehyde. Tolerates low light.
  • Parlor Palm (Chamaedorea elegans): Removes formaldehyde. Low light tolerant. Compact size.
  • Broadleaf Lady Palm: Removes ammonia. Slow-growing. Elegant appearance.
  • Barberton Daisy (Gerbera jamesonii): Removes benzene and trichloroethylene. Needs bright direct light.
  • Cast Iron Plant (Aspidistra elatior): Nearly indestructible. Tolerates deep shade, neglect, and drought.

Pet Safety Strategy

Even non-toxic plants can cause mild stomach upset if a pet eats large quantities. Place plants on high shelves, in hanging baskets, or in rooms that pets do not access unsupervised. Cat grass (wheatgrass) costs $3-5 and gives cats a safe chewing alternative that redirects them away from your houseplants. If a pet ingests a toxic plant, call the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at 888-426-4435 immediately.

Room-by-Room Placement Guide

Bedroom

Best plants: Snake Plant, Peace Lily, Lavender. The Snake Plant is ideal because it converts CO2 to oxygen at night (most plants only do this during the day). Place 2 medium Snake Plants in a standard bedroom for measurable nighttime air quality improvement. The Peace Lily adds formaldehyde removal and flowers even in low bedroom light.

Living Room

Best plants: Areca Palm, Rubber Plant, Dracaena, English Ivy. The living room typically has the most furniture, carpet, and electronics, which means the most VOC off-gassing. Large plants like the Areca Palm (6 feet) provide substantial air purification and natural humidification. Place 3-4 plants in a typical living room for noticeable impact.

Kitchen

Best plants: Pothos, Aloe Vera, Spider Plant. The kitchen benefits from plants that tolerate humidity fluctuations and handle the ammonia from cleaning products. Pothos trails beautifully from the top of cabinets. Aloe Vera sits perfectly on a sunny windowsill and provides burn relief for cooking accidents. Spider Plants handle the variable conditions well.

Bathroom

Best plants: Boston Fern, Pothos, ZZ Plant. Bathrooms have the humidity that Boston Ferns crave. Pothos thrives in the variable light of most bathrooms. The ZZ Plant handles the low light of windowless bathrooms. Even a single plant in the bathroom removes formaldehyde from cleaning products and personal care items.

Home Office

Best plants: Snake Plant, ZZ Plant, Bamboo Palm. Offices need plants that tolerate artificial light, air conditioning, and potential neglect during busy periods. Studies show that having plants visible during work reduces stress by 37% and increases productivity by 15%. Place at least 2 plants within your line of sight.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many air-purifying plants do you need per room?

NASA recommended one plant per 100 square feet. For a 200 sq ft bedroom, 2 plants. For a 300 sq ft living room, 3 plants. Recent research suggests plants supplement but do not replace mechanical air filtration. Even 2-3 plants per room measurably reduce VOCs and increase humidity by 5-10%.

Which air-purifying plants are safe for cats and dogs?

ASPCA-confirmed pet-safe options: Spider Plant, Boston Fern, Areca Palm, Bamboo Palm, Parlor Palm, Broadleaf Lady Palm, Barberton Daisy, and Cast Iron Plant. Many popular air purifiers (Peace Lily, Pothos, Snake Plant) are toxic to pets. Always check the ASPCA database before buying.

Do indoor plants really clean the air or is it a myth?

Plants do remove certain toxins, but the effect is more modest than early NASA research suggested in sealed chambers. In real rooms with normal air exchange, you would need hundreds of plants to match a HEPA purifier. Plants provide measurable but modest air quality improvements. Their proven benefits for humidity, stress reduction, and well-being are significant and well-documented.

What are the easiest air-purifying plants for beginners?

The five easiest are Snake Plant, Pothos, ZZ Plant, Spider Plant, and Rubber Plant. All tolerate neglect, irregular watering, and variable light. If you have never kept a plant alive, start with a Snake Plant or Pothos. They are the most forgiving plants available.

Can air-purifying plants help with allergies?

Plants can help by removing VOCs and increasing humidity, which reduces airborne dust. However, plants can also trigger allergies through soil mold or pollen. If you have mold allergies, use well-draining soil and avoid overwatering. For pollen allergies, choose non-flowering plants. A HEPA purifier is more effective for allergies, but plants provide complementary benefits.

Do air-purifying plants work in rooms with no windows?

Yes, but your options are limited. Snake Plants, ZZ Plants, Pothos, and Cast Iron Plants tolerate very low light including rooms with only artificial lighting. They grow more slowly in low light but still purify air. Use a grow light ($15-30) to supplement if you want faster growth and healthier plants in windowless rooms.
